What is a kilonova?

A kilonova is a merger between two neutron stars or a neutron star and a black hole. The Kilonova Data Challenge primarily focuses on modeling the binary neutron star type of kilonova. The most famous kilonova to date is the event called GW170817. It is famous for being the first gravitational wave detection detected by non-gravitational wave detectors- that is, it was detected in electromagnetic spectrum observations as well.

For more info on kilonovae and transients, check out the Astrobites Guide to Transient Astronomy. You should read through the sections on kilonovae (singular KN, plural KNe) and supernovae. While we are not searching for supernovae directly, it is important to know the distinction between them and kilonovae, as we will likely come across them in our KN search.

What are Right Ascension and Declination?

Right ascension and declination are two dimensions of the equatorial coordinate system, which is most commonly used to refer to the positions of astronomical objects. Its origin is Earth's center, which makes it a geocentric coordinate system. It is also a spherical coordinate system, rather than rectangular. Right ascension is the equivalent to longitude, and declination is the equivalent to latitude.

  • Right ascension is measured in hours, minutes, and seconds. There are 60 minutes in an hour and 60 seconds in a minute, the same way time is measured. These smaller units are also commonly called arcminutes and arcseconds, to differentiate them from units of time measurement. This means that a perfect circle is 24 hours. In other words, a single hour of right ascension is equivalent to 360°⁄24h= 15°. An arcminute is 1⁄60 of an hour, or 1⁄1440 of a circle. An arcsecond is 1⁄3600 of an hour, or 1⁄86400 of a circle.
    As an example, the moon is approximately 31 arcminutes in size, or a little more than 1⁄2 of an hour.
    Hours are typically denoted by the letter h, arcminutes by the symbol ', and arcseconds by the symbol ".
  • Declination is measured in the same way, but comprises the vertical axis of the celestial coordinate system: it is the celestial equivalent to latitude.

Using Python

The Rubin Science Platform will be sufficiently useful for most cases. However, if you ever need to work where there is a poor or insecure Internet connection, or you want to code on your own machine, you will need to install a distribution of Python.

  • If you are completely new to Python, this website has many tutorials that will guide you through the basics. You should work through all the pages listed under the "Learn the Basics" and "Data Science Tutorials" categories.
  • Anaconda Navigator
    • Anaconda Navigator is a distribution of Python that comes pre-installed with many commonly used scientific software packages. Through it, you can run code directly in your browser! You can install it here.
